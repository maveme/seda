Software Engineering and Digital Arts Conservation (SEDA) Workshop

March 25, 2026

Amsterdam, The Netherlands

Introduction

Digital art builds on top of rich stacks of digital technology. Blending multiple media such as code, data, signals, and artificial intelligence; software artists integrate various types of software to draw, animate, control, or synchronize different parts of an artwork. Software-based art has entered our artistic heritage in the 1960's with the early Experiments in Art and Technology and the computer art of pioneers such as Vera Molnar. Since then, it has kept growing up to the most recent NFT collections, immersive works by Refik Anadol or Sasha Stiles' poetry fueled by generative AI.

Software and artificial intelligence open fantastic opportunities to amplify formal creativity, letting artists experiment with novel media, and new production techniques at the interface of science, technology and art. Yet, software artworks are also highly vulnerable . Rapid software evolution, hardware obsolescence, insufficient documentation, and fragile software supply chains threaten their longevity, reproducibility, and cultural value.

This one-day Software Engineering and Digital Art Conservation (SEDA) workshop seeks to convene researchers to share knowledge about their SEDA experiences from the Software Engineering and the Conservation perspectives. This SEDA workshop will feature researchers and practitioners with interest in the intersection of software engineering and digital art conservation, preservation, and history. A key goal of this workshop is to foster discussion and facilitate future SEDA collaborations. As such, ample time will be provided for discussion and brainstorming between participants. ZKM | Center for Art and Media Karlsruhe

Marc Schütze will present the Exhibition VM Controller, an open-source framework for preserving and displaying historical digital artworks that depend on obsolete technology. The system uses virtual machines with automated snapshot-based recovery to keep legacy software running reliably without human intervention in museum and gallery settings.
